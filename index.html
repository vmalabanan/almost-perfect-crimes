Almost Perfect Crimes: A Lesson from Death in Paradise

Great mystery stories typically involve compelling characters, a seemingly impossible crime, twisty trails of clues and red herrings, and a solution that feels surprising, inevitable, and most of all, satisfying. In this post we’ll explore the almost perfect crime through the lens of the most popular programs on British television.

DS Florence Cassell and DI Humphrey Goodman solve seemingly impossible murders in a picturesque Caribbean landscape (image: BBC)

The blueprint for most mysteries—and for every Death in Paradise episode—is this:

  1. A short introduction to the protagonist/cast of characters going about their lives in the time before the mystery is revealed.
  2. A mystery is presented—often murder and/or a seemingly impossible crime.
  3. The protagonist gradually unravels the who, what, when, where, and why of the crime, obtaining information that sheds new light on the crime, victim, and suspects. The protagonist typically chases some false leads and red herrings.
  4. The protagonist solves the crime.
  5. Story-world matters not related to the crime are wrapped up.

Traditionally, the protagonist solves the crime. For this to happen, the crime must be almost perfect—that's because a perfect crime would be, by definition, unsolvable. To put it another way: imperfections in the way the crime was carried out generate the clues the protagonist needs to solve the crime.

In Death in Paradise, the murderers always make a mistake, usually following into the following categories:

  • Poor planning—or, more often: naiveite. The perpetrator tends to believe that no one else will notice tiny clues left at the crime scene (these are often discrepancies: things that should be there but aren't, or things that shouldn't be there but are). However, the protagonist almost always notices these hard-to-spot details (eventually).
  • For spur-of-the-moment crimes: lack of time or options means the perpetrator has less optimal options for weapon disposal, etc. Things get messy.
  • Things not going according to plan—for example, unaccounted for witnesses.

The Scene

Detective Inspector Humphrey Goodman is having dinner at a hotel and notices one of the guests drunkenly stumble back to his room. The hotel is a family affair, run by the man's brother and sister-in-law, and was the brothers' childhood home. The man hasn't set foot in the house in 20 years and has just come from the kitchen and is carrying a plate with a sandwich on it.

Ten minutes later, the housekeeper finds the man stabbed to death in his room. No one else is around. His watch and wallet are missing, the murder weapon is nowhere to be found, and his balcony doors are flung open.

DI Goodman had a clear view of the one staircase leading to the man's room, and besides the housekeeper, no one went up or down the stairs after the man went up. His room is the only one at the top of the stairs.

The Clues

  • The ground beneath the man's balcony is undisturbed (no footprints).
  • A decades-old pack of cigarettes is sitting on the victim's bedside table. The cigarettes are too old to smoke, no one recognizes the pack, and those close to the victim say he hasn't smoked since he was a teenager. His mother had disapproved of the habit.
  • Things not going according to plan—for example, unaccounted for witnesses.

In an episode of Death in Paradise, an old pack of cigarettes found in the victim’s room is a clue that helps unravel the mystery of who killed him and why. (image: eBay)

The Solution

The man had been stabbed by his sister-in-law in the kitchen. They were secret lovers, and there were extenuating circumstances to the stabbing. In his dying moments, the man tried to divert attention away from her: he thew the knife in the dishwasher, used her scarf to control the bleeding, and walked back with the plated sandwich to make it seem to anyone watching that he was okay (and only drunk, not stabbed). Once upstairs, he set the stage for a robbery gone wrong: he flung open the balcony doors, then hid his watch, wallet, and the scarf in a secret hiding place in the baseboards—which he’d used as a teenager to hide packs of cigarettes.

Here's where the almost perfect part comes in: there was still an old pack of cigarettes in the hiding space, which he needed to take out to make everything else fit. This clue helps DI Goodman realize the crime could've only been committed one way. Without it, we'd still have the undisturbed ground below the balcony pointing to the crime being committed by someone still in the hotel, but that wouldn't be enough to narrow things down. We'd need other clues.

Of course, the story could have been done countless ways with other sets of clues, but the lesson here is this: the protagonist needs clues to solve the crime. They need an almost perfect murder.
